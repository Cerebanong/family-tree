## Interesting Facts
Mark Frost was born on April 21, 1818, in Belgrade, Kennebec County, Maine, the son of Benjamin Frost. He grew up in central Maine's farming country and married Catherine E. Gibberd on March 11, 1847, in Skowhegan, Somerset County. The couple settled into a life of farming, first in the Kilmarnock area (where they appear in the 1850 census) and later in Medford, Piscataquis County, where they were established by 1860. Together they raised seven children: Sarah, William, James, Clara, John Lyman, Etta Clarista, and Nellie Burton.

When the Civil War broke out, Mark answered the call despite being 41 years old — well above the typical enlistment age. He mustered in on April 30, 1861, at Medford and served as a cook's assistant, supporting the troops from behind the front lines. After his initial service, he enlisted a second time on December 23, 1863, this time in Sidney, Kennebec County, suggesting he had returned to his birth region. His willingness to serve twice speaks to a deep sense of duty during a turbulent era in American history.

Mark's later years remain partially obscured by the passage of time. By 1870, Catherine appears to have been residing in Augusta — possibly already at the Maine Insane Hospital where she would spend decades. A Maine Deaths and Burials record places Mark's death on March 19, 1877, in Augusta, Kennebec County, at approximately age 58. He is believed to be buried at Hill Crest Cemetery in Lagrange, the same cemetery where several of his children and grandchildren would later rest, binding the Frost family to this small Penobscot County community for generations.

Mark's legacy lives on through his descendants. His son John Lyman Frost settled in Bangor and became a direct ancestor of the family. His children scattered across Maine and New England — Clara married into the Hubbard family, Etta became a schoolteacher in Connecticut, and Nellie was adopted and raised in Massachusetts. Despite the hardships of frontier farming and war, Mark and Catherine's family endured and grew across the region.
