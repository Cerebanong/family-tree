## Interesting Facts
John Bialy (later Bailey) was born in 1881 in the Prussian Section of Poland. While he was still a child, his mother and father were taken away on a one-way trip to Siberia. His brothers and sisters were divided up among relatives; later, all of his siblings were killed. John himself witnessed one of his brothers being taken for a walk and then shot. The relatives who took him in treated him as a nuisance, and he left on his own at the age of 12.

As a young teenager, John attempted to learn a trade, apprenticing successively in a shoe shop, with a tailor, and with a blacksmith. In each case, he found himself doing domestic chores rather than learning the trade. At the blacksmith's, he was expected to watch five children, repair things around the house, and change diapers, while only occasionally being allowed into the shop. At suppertime, he watched butter piled high on every slice of bread but his own. Each time, he simply walked away.

He then worked in a bakery for 15 cents a day, stealing food to keep himself fed. When the total thefts were investigated, the other workers blamed everything on John, and he lost his job. He moved to the mines, starting at 25 cents a day. There was a cave-in and he was hurt, but he recovered and returned to work. When he demanded equal pay of 75 cents a day — the wage paid to married men doing the same work — the foreman refused, saying 'John, you aren't married. What would you do with all that money?' So he walked away.

John joined a band of smugglers, running whiskey, books, and underground literature across the border into the Russian sector. He made more money than he ever had, but was injured again when a border guard struck him over the head with a gun stock. At 19, facing compulsory army service, he refused to fight for a government whose oppression of the Polish people he despised. He could not accept the teachings from the pulpit that the government's executions, confiscations, and oppression were 'the Will of God.' As he said: 'To me, what is not good for the people is not, and never will be, the Will of God.'

One night, John swam across a river and began his slow journey to England, sleeping in woods, haystacks, and on porches. He stayed in England for about a year, but work was scarce among the many Polish youths fleeing persecution.

He left for America, arriving in New York City in March 1902. He slept on the docks where rats ran everywhere and would bite if not scared off. He worked as a gardener, then took a job in a stable with 150 horses, caring for 45 of them — feeding, watering, currying, harnessing, cleaning stalls — a 24-hour, 7-day-a-week job paying $7.00 per week. He slept in the stables because he couldn't afford a room, and his clothes permanently smelled of horse. When the foreman kept 'borrowing' money and refusing to return it, and then fired John for demanding it back, he walked away once more.

After a Turkish bath and new clothes, John read about the building of the Lehigh Valley Railroad Shops in Sayre, Pennsylvania, jumped on a train, and arrived in May 1903. He started at 13 cents an hour on piecework, earning about $26 a month. On May 14, 1906, he married Elizabeth Malanowski, and they lived on Cayuta Avenue for one year before buying a house on Providence Street for $1,400 in 1907. From 1908 to 1924, they had six children, beginning with Harriet.

On April 18, 1924, John took his wife Elizabeth to Binghamton, where she died at approximately age 38, leaving him with $3,700 in the bank to cover the mortgage, lawyers, food, clothing, and bills for his family. He continued working at the railroad, and after 39 years and 10 months of service, retired in 1942 earning 86 cents an hour. He began drawing $48.95 per month in retirement benefits — there had been no Social Security when he first started.

In his final years, John expressed quiet contentment: 'I have my own home. It isn't much. I know I haven't much longer to live. And I just want to stay here to the end.' He dictated his life story to his granddaughter Joan Shedden on March 24, 1969, and died on August 7, 1970, in Sayre, Pennsylvania.
