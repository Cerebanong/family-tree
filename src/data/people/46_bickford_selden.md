## Interesting Facts
Selden Foss Bickford was born on February 13, 1862, in Barton, Orleans County, Vermont, the son of Harris S. Bickford and Celinda E. Foss. His middle name honored his maternal grandfather, Seldon Foss (1802–1891), a long-lived Vermont farmer who would survive to see his grandson well into adulthood. The Bickford family had deep roots in northeastern Vermont, and Selden's early childhood reflected the restless energy of post-Civil War America: in 1870, when Selden was just eight years old, the family had relocated to Granada, Nemaha County, Kansas — part of the great westward migration of New England families seeking new farmland on the Great Plains. But the experiment was short-lived, and by 1880 the Bickfords had returned to Barton, Vermont, where Selden grew up.

On June 21, 1887, Selden married Annie S. Fay in Vermont. Annie was the daughter of Michael L. Fay — a Civil War veteran who had come to Vermont from Quebec — and Harriet N. Roberts. The couple settled in Barton, where their two daughters were born: Muriel Helena in 1891 and Mildred K. in 1894. Selden lost his father Harris in 1883 (when Selden was 21) and his mother Celinda in 1894, the same year his second daughter was born. Despite these losses, the Bickford household appears in the 1900 census still rooted in Barton.

The family's trajectory changed dramatically in the early 20th century. By 1910 Selden and Annie were living in Kent, Delaware, apparently with their son-in-law Roswell Lewis. By 1915 they had moved to Franklin Township, Gloucester County, New Jersey, and by 1920 they had settled in Washington, D.C., where they would remain for two decades. Selden's D.C. residency through the 1920s and 1930s — during the era of the New Deal and federal expansion — suggests he may have found employment in the federal civil service, a common path for skilled workers who relocated to the capital in that era.

Annie died on November 22, 1947, leaving Selden a widower after sixty years of marriage. By 1950, at age 88, he had moved to Woodbury in Deptford Township, Gloucester County, New Jersey — likely to be near one of his daughters. He died in 1952, having lived through nine decades of American history from the aftermath of the Civil War through the beginning of the Cold War. He was laid to rest in Barton, Vermont, the town where his journey had begun.
